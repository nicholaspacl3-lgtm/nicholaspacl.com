Social Mechanical Optimization

The DNA of Business Systems

Nicholas Pacl
• April 21, 2026 • 20 min read

IThe Two Inheritances

Every business system has a DNA that encodes what it believes the primary throughput variable to be. In the East, that variable is harmony. In the West, it is a step in the process. These are not equivalent framings expressed in different languages. They are genuinely different theories of what a production system is, and they produce genuinely different firms.

The Eastern inheritance is most rigorously articulated in the Japanese knowledge-creation tradition, where Nonaka and Takeuchi argued that the competitive advantage of Japanese firms came from their capacity to continuously convert tacit, relational, bodily knowledge into explicit, transferable form and back again (Nonaka & Takeuchi, 1995). The throughput variable in that system is not a machine cycle time. It is the quality of the relational substrate — what Nonaka later called ba — through which socialization, externalization, combination, and internalization can occur. The firm is a field of shared experience before it is a sequence of operations. If the field is disturbed, throughput collapses, because the medium through which knowledge moves has been damaged. Hofstede's cross-cultural work puts numbers on the same intuition: East Asian cultures score high on collectivism, long-term orientation, and power distance, which means the firm is experienced as a tightly-knit crystal lattice rather than a loose gas of individuals (Hofstede, Hofstede & Minkov, 2010).

The Western inheritance runs in the other direction. From Taylor's scientific management through Goldratt's Theory of Constraints, the throughput variable is a step in the process. Goldratt's formulation is canonical: every system has a constraint, the constraint is a specific operation or resource, and the entire firm should subordinate itself to the exploitation of that constraint until throughput is maximized (Goldratt & Cox, 1984). The firm is a directed graph of operations, and the managerial task is to find the slowest node and widen it. People appear in this framing mostly as inputs to, or operators of, the steps. Relationships are not the substrate of production; they are overhead on top of it.

Both inheritances are correct about something, and both are incomplete. The Eastern model underestimates the extent to which harmony without codification is illegible — it produces firms that cannot scale across cultures because their throughput is locked inside a relational container that does not export. The Western model underestimates the extent to which the process steps are themselves enacted by a relational system that can either absorb or reject change. A perfectly engineered step in a socially unprepared firm produces the same thing a bottleneck does: missed throughput.

The actual DNA of a business system is neither Eastern nor Western. It is the intersection of the two.

The thesis of this essay is that the actual DNA of a business system is neither. It is the intersection of the two. You cannot optimize only for the harmony of people, and you cannot optimize only for the harmony of machines. You must optimize for both, simultaneously, and the degree to which a firm does this determines the ceiling of its throughput. I call the framework that takes this joint problem seriously Social Mechanical Optimization (SMO), and I want to argue that it is not a metaphor — it is a formal joint-optimization problem with a specific structure, a specific Nash equilibrium, and a specific engineering intervention that raises the equilibrium.

IIThe Sociotechnical Lineage

The claim that the social and technical systems of a firm must be jointly optimized is not new. It was articulated seventy-five years ago by Trist and Bamforth (1951) in their study of the Longwall method of coal mining in Britain. Their finding was that the mechanization of the coal face, which was on paper a pure productivity upgrade, destroyed the small face-to-face work group that had made the hand-got method effective. The new technical system was superior, the new social system was inferior, and the net result was worse performance, more internal conflict, and higher worker resistance. From this, Trist and his colleagues at the Tavistock Institute derived the principle of joint optimization: the performance of the whole system is maximized only when the social and technical subsystems are designed together, and the optimal state of the whole typically requires a less-than-optimal state for each subsystem considered alone (Trist, 1981; Mohrman, Ramstad & Zheng, 2018).

Sociotechnical systems theory (STS) is the intellectual ancestor of SMO, and SMO should be read as a direct extension of it into the age of AI agents. But STS, as originally formulated, has a limitation that matters. It describes the joint-optimization problem descriptively, and it prescribes design principles — responsible autonomy, adaptability, meaningful tasks (Cherns, 1976) — but it does not give us a formal object. It does not tell us what the equilibrium looks like, how to measure distance from it, or how to compute the marginal throughput gain of a given intervention. The conversation has stayed at the level of case study and principle, which is one reason STS lost ground in the 1990s to lean and six sigma — movements that offered cleaner metrics even though they were sociotechnically impoverished.

The two other pillars SMO rests on are Cohen and Levinthal's (1990) concept of absorptive capacity and Edmondson's (1999) work on psychological safety. Cohen and Levinthal argued that a firm's ability to assimilate external knowledge is a function of its prior related knowledge, and that this capacity is cumulative and path-dependent — firms that fail to invest in absorptive capacity in one period become locked out of the next. Edmondson demonstrated empirically that team psychological safety, the shared belief that it is safe to take interpersonal risks, is the mediating variable between structural design and team learning — and that without it, even well-designed teams do not convert information into performance. Read together, these two literatures are saying the same thing in different vocabularies: there is a rate at which a relational system can absorb change, and that rate is the real governor on throughput.

SMO is what you get when you fuse these three lineages — Trist's joint optimization, Cohen and Levinthal's absorptive capacity, and Edmondson's psychological safety — and ask what the formal equilibrium looks like when you do.

IIITSMC Arizona as the Canonical Case

The TSMC Arizona fab is the cleanest contemporary example of the SMO problem, because it is a case where the technical system is world-leading and the relational system is a cross-cultural transplant, and the two were not jointly designed.

The technical system TSMC is moving to Phoenix is the product of four decades of refinement in Taiwan. It is, by any reasonable measure, the most sophisticated semiconductor manufacturing process in the world. The fab depends on keeping capital equipment running continuously to avoid billions of dollars in scrapped wafers and lost throughput (Yeh, 2022). In pure Goldratt terms, the cycle time of the constraint tools is the throughput variable, and the firm should subordinate everything to it. Taiwan does exactly this: engineers are on call, shifts are long, hierarchy is steep, meetings are frequent, and obedience to the process is high.

In Arizona, the same technical system met a different relational system. American engineers described rigid, counterproductive hierarchies; Taiwanese veterans described the Americans as lacking dedication; meeting loads and work expectations became flashpoints (Zhou, 2024). Thirteen former employees filed suit alleging an anti-American culture. The company has reduced meetings, invested in communications training for managers, and publicly acknowledged that Taiwan practice cannot simply be transplanted (Everington, 2024). A TSMC director of employee relations at the Arizona site put the learning directly: what works in Taiwan does not automatically work in Phoenix.

Cultural friction at TSMC Arizona is not separate from throughput. It is throughput, manifesting as delayed ramp, higher turnover, longer time-to-yield.

The standard framings of this story — culture clash, HR problem, labor relations — all treat the social system as an obstacle to the real work, which is the mechanical throughput of the fab. This is precisely the Western error. Cultural friction at TSMC Arizona is not separate from throughput. It is throughput, manifesting as delayed ramp, higher turnover, longer time-to-yield, and the slower diffusion of the tacit process knowledge that Nonaka and Takeuchi identified as the real asset being transferred. The Arizona fab is a sociotechnical system in which the technical subsystem was specified at world-class and the social subsystem was specified by default. Joint optimization was not attempted. The result is exactly what Trist's framework predicts: the fab is not yielding what either the equipment or the people are individually capable of.

This is the motivating case for SMO. The question the framework is designed to answer is: if you could specify the relational system of a cross-border fab with the same engineering rigor you specify the lithography process, what would the specification look like, and what throughput would you unlock?

IVThe Nash Equilibrium of a Business System

To see why SMO is a joint-optimization problem and not a sequential one, it helps to model a firm as a two-player coordination game between the social system S and the mechanical system M. Each subsystem chooses a level of agentic energy — the rate at which it introduces change, new demands, new tools, new processes — and a level of absorptive protocol — the codified and enacted infrastructure through which change is received and metabolized.

M: Low Energy M: High Energy
S: Low Absorption (2, 2) Stable, stagnant — the mature bureaucracy (1, 4)* Burnout, quality collapse — the TSMC Arizona cell
S: High Absorption (4, 1)* Slack, underutilized — culture without ambition (5, 5) SMO equilibrium: harmony + velocity, maximum throughput

The upper-left cell is the stagnant firm: neither the technical system nor the social system is pushing, and throughput is low but stable. This is the default state of a mature bureaucracy. The lower-right is what we want: high agentic energy on the mechanical side — new tools, new agents, new processes — matched by a high-absorption social system that can metabolize the change without fracturing. This is where throughput is maximized.

The two off-diagonal cells are the interesting ones, because they are the equilibria that firms actually land in. The upper-right cell is the TSMC Arizona cell: the mechanical system demands high velocity, the social system cannot absorb it, and the firm pays in burnout, turnover, quality incidents, and lawsuits. The lower-left cell is the opposite pathology: a firm with excellent culture and psychological safety but a timid technical roadmap — the relational system has capacity that is never called upon. In my experience this is the modal failure mode of companies that have taken psychological safety seriously but have not married it to mechanical ambition.

The critical property of this matrix is that the (5, 5) cell is unreachable by unilateral moves. If M unilaterally raises energy, the firm slides into the upper-right cell and performance collapses. If S unilaterally raises absorption, it sits in the lower-left with slack that the mechanical system never uses. The cell is only reachable through coordinated, protocol-mediated co-movement. This is the precise sense in which SMO is a Nash equilibrium problem: the payoff-dominant cell is not the risk-dominant cell, and moving the firm to the payoff-dominant cell requires an institution — a protocol — that makes the coordinated move common knowledge.

This is where SMO departs from sociotechnical theory as it has been written to date. STS correctly identifies that joint optimization is the goal, but it leaves unanswered how coordination is achieved under uncertainty. SMO answers that the coordination device is a codified protocol that expands absorptive capacity at a calibrated rate, and that releases mechanical energy only as absorption is demonstrated. In the language of Cohen and Levinthal (1990), it is an absorptive-capacity-first approach to change, in which the rate of agentic energy is governed by the current rate of organizational assimilation rather than by the ambition of the technical roadmap.

VThe Turbocharger Analogy

The engineering intuition I want to borrow is the turbocharger. A naturally aspirated engine is limited by how much air it can draw in on its own intake stroke. A turbocharger uses exhaust pressure to force more air into the cylinder than the engine could aspirate unaided, raising peak power without changing the displacement. But a turbocharger requires two things: a compressor that can deliver higher-pressure air, and an intake system that can withstand it. If you bolt a turbocharger onto a block that was not designed for the pressure, you do not get more power. You get a fractured block.

AI agents are the turbocharger. The mechanical side is ready. What is not ready, in most firms, is the intake.

AI agents are the turbocharger. They represent an unprecedented increase in the rate at which mechanical energy can be introduced into a firm — tasks that took a quarter can be drafted in a day, analyses that took a team can be run by one operator, and the number of parallel workstreams a single human can supervise is now an order of magnitude higher than it was in 2022. The mechanical side is ready. What is not ready, in most firms, is the intake. The relational system — the meetings, the reviews, the approval chains, the trust relationships, the psychological safety to flag a bad agent output without career risk — has not been re-engineered to receive the higher pressure.

This is the diagnosis SMO makes of the current moment. The modal firm is under-absorbing agentic energy and will continue to do so until the relational system is explicitly engineered to receive it. The question is not whether to deploy agents. The question is at what rate, and with what protocol, and through what absorption interface.

SMO proposes that the turbocharger of business throughput is a protocol that (a) introduces agentic energy at the rate the relational system can absorb, and (b) actively expands that absorption capacity through codified mechanisms. The two halves of this protocol are inseparable. A protocol that only governs rate becomes a brake. A protocol that only expands capacity without governing rate produces burnout. SMO does both, and the specific claim of the framework is that a firm operating under an SMO protocol reaches a higher equilibrium throughput than a firm using either Eastern harmony-first management or Western process-first management alone.

VIThesis Deliverables

The thesis this essay introduces will produce three artifacts, each addressing a different level of the framework.

The first is the formal SMO model. This will specify the sociotechnical joint-optimization problem as a two-system control problem with state variables for absorptive capacity, agentic energy rate, and throughput, and with transition functions derived from the empirical literatures on sociotechnical design, absorptive capacity, and psychological safety. The model will formalize the intuition of the Nash matrix in Section IV into a continuous-state dynamical system whose equilibria can be computed and whose sensitivity to protocol design can be characterized.

The second is a set of empirical simulation results run with AI agents. Given that the thesis is being written during a period in which agentic systems are rapidly becoming available as instrumented research subjects, it is possible to run SMO scenarios at meaningful scale. The simulation platform will instantiate firms as populations of agents with configurable absorption, energy, and protocol parameters, and will measure throughput outcomes under different protocol regimes, including no-protocol (the TSMC Arizona baseline), harmony-first, process-first, and full SMO. The goal of the simulation is not to prove SMO's superiority in the abstract but to characterize the conditions under which it dominates the other regimes and the conditions under which it does not.

The third is an operating document translating SMO into implementable protocols. The framework is only useful if it crosses the gap from formal model to daily practice, which is the gap where most sociotechnical theory has historically failed. The operating document will specify, at the level of meeting cadences, review rituals, escalation paths, and agent-deployment gates, what an SMO-compliant firm actually does on a Monday morning. It will be written for a corporate planner at a facility like ESMC Dresden or TSMC Arizona, where the joint-optimization problem is not hypothetical but in front of them right now.

VIIClosing

Every business system has DNA that encodes what it believes the primary throughput variable to be. Firms that inherit only the Eastern strand optimize for a harmony they cannot export. Firms that inherit only the Western strand optimize for steps that their people will not enact. The firms that will define the next two decades — the firms running cross-border fabs, the firms integrating AI agents at scale, the firms operating at the seam between high-trust and high-velocity — will be the firms whose DNA encodes both strands and whose protocols make the coordinated move common knowledge.

Social Mechanical Optimization is the framework that names this problem, formalizes it, and, I will argue in the chapters that follow, solves enough of it to be useful on Monday morning.
